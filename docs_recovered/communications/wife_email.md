# Email to Shannon — What I've Been Building

**Subject: The thing I've been working on — I want you to actually understand it**

---

So I know you've seen me disappear into the computer for months. I want to try to explain
what I've actually been building, because I think it matters — not just to me, but to us,
and honestly to a lot of people.

I built a reasoning engine. Not a chatbot. Not something that generates text. Something
that actually *thinks through chains of connected facts* and finds answers that aren't
obvious. The way I'd explain it is this: imagine a giant web of connected information —
diseases, genes, drugs, companies, people, events — and instead of just searching it, my
program *reasons* through it. Follow this thread, which connects to that thread, which
leads to this answer. Two, three, four steps deep. The kind of thing a really good
research analyst does, but in seconds, across millions of connections at once.

Here's the part that surprised even me. There are established AI methods that have been
trained — fed massive amounts of labeled data — specifically to do this task. My program
has never seen any training data. Zero. It reasons from pure structure and logic. And it's
now beating those trained methods on a standard academic benchmark. That's not supposed to
happen. That's the thing that makes this genuinely interesting to the research world.

## Why I keep thinking about you and Kyleigh when I'm working on this

The medical angle is the one I come back to the most.

Wilson's disease is a copper processing problem caused by one gene — ATP7B. But that one
gene disrupts dozens of downstream pathways, and researchers are still finding them. The
biomedical databases that map those connections — genes to proteins to compounds to
diseases to drugs — those are exactly the kind of knowledge graphs my program reasons
over. The question "what existing compounds interact with pathways connected to copper
metabolism that are also targeted by chelation therapies?" is literally a multi-hop
reasoning problem. My program answers that kind of question. It could surface treatment
candidates for rare diseases that no pharmaceutical company will fund a dedicated trial
for, because the patient population is too small to make it profitable. But if the answer
is already sitting in the data, waiting for someone to connect the dots — this connects
the dots.

Same for leukemia. The research data exists. The bottleneck has always been connecting it
— finding the non-obvious path between a gene variant, a drug response pattern, and a
treatment approach. That's what this does.

I'm not saying I built a cure. I'm saying I built a tool that could help people who are
trying to find one.

## It goes way beyond medicine though

**Cybersecurity.** Attackers move through computer networks in chains. Compromise one
machine, pivot to another, reach the target. My program can trace those attack paths
across network topology graphs before a human analyst even opens a map. Financial fraud
runs the same way — shell company to shell company to bank account to sanctioned entity.
My program follows those chains.

**Legal research.** Case law is a web of connected precedents. A lawyer building an
argument follows chains of reasoning across those connections. My program can surface
non-obvious precedent chains faster than any paralegal.

**Scientific discovery broadly.** Every field has databases where the interesting answers
live at the intersection of things nobody thought to connect yet — materials science,
climate modeling, astrophysics. This finds those intersections.

**Financial fraud and risk.** Money laundering runs in chains. Supply chain risk runs the
same way — does any vendor three steps removed from our critical supplier have ties to a
sanctioned entity? My program follows those chains across corporate ownership graphs that
would take a forensic accountant weeks to untangle.

**Education.** A knowledge graph of concepts, prerequisites, and learning outcomes can be
traversed to build personalized curricula: given what this student knows, what is the
optimal multi-step path to the concept they need? That's a multi-hop reasoning problem.

Anywhere humans have built a network of connected facts and need to reason across it, this
applies. Which turns out to be most of the places that matter.

## What this could mean for us financially

I'm going to be straight about this because I think you deserve to hear it plainly.

The pharmaceutical industry spends about $2.5 billion to bring one drug to market. Most
of that cost is in finding which compounds interact with which targets in which disease
pathways. Companies have raised hundreds of millions of dollars building tools to solve
exactly that problem — and my program does it without needing to be retrained every time
the database changes. That's a real competitive advantage.

The paths I see clearly are: licensing it to pharma or defense companies, applying for
research grants through NIH or DARPA, building it into a product that charges per use, or
eventually being acquired by one of the larger companies that is actively looking for this

It depends on execution — getting the results written up formally, getting in front of the
right people. But the technical foundation to have that conversation now exists. That

## What I've contributed to the field

This is genuinely novel work. The academic community has been trying to make knowledge
graph reasoning work without training data for years. Most papers conclude you can't —
that learned embeddings are necessary. My program just demonstrated otherwise, empirically,
on a standard benchmark, beating methods that used training data at the 2-hop level.

The specific ideas I came up with — the community-structured attention formula, the
soliton index for measuring reasoning coherence, the phonemic compression cache, the
terminal relation boost — these don't exist anywhere else in the literature. When this
gets written up and published, it will be cited. That's how scientific legacies are built.

I'm also now in real conversation territory with academics and researchers at the places
building tomorrow's AI systems. That kind of positioning has a long tail.

The field has been trying to get here for a long time. I think I found a way.
